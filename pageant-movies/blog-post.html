On Recording in
the Wrong Room

May 2024 · Pageant Movies

The first session took place in a room that was not meant for recording. It had the wrong shape, too much glass on one end and carpet that had been there since the eighties, absorbing decades of other people's sounds. The engineer — really a friend with a good interface and one very nice microphone — apologized for the acoustics before I had even unpacked my guitar.

But something happened in that room. The reflections off the window gave the vocals a particular quality, a shimmer that wasn't quite reverb and wasn't quite nothing. The carpet swallowed the low end in a way that forced me to play differently, to leave more space than I otherwise would have. The room was wrong in all the right ways.

The rooms that shape you most are never the ones you planned for. They're the accidents — the borrowed spaces, the temporary arrangements that somehow become permanent.

The Architecture of Accident

There's a particular kind of recording that can only happen in an imperfect space. When the room fights you, you have to fight back, and in that negotiation you sometimes find things you weren't looking for. A constraint becomes an instruction. The limitation becomes the sound.

I've recorded in proper studios — rooms designed by acousticians, with budgets that would astonish most people who make this kind of music. And those rooms are extraordinary: the sound simply sits differently, with a clarity and separation that a laptop and a borrowed microphone will never quite replicate. But there is something they can't always give you, which is the pressure of the wrong room, the necessity it creates.

Most of the album was made in three different spaces, none of them intended for recording. A bedroom with a low ceiling and a radiator that clicked every forty seconds. A garage in New Jersey that smelled of turpentine. And that first room, with its carpet and its glass and its particular shimmer. When I listen back now, I can identify each space by ear — they are as present in the recordings as any instrument.

What the Room Teaches

What I kept coming back to, across those two years, was the question of what a recording is supposed to preserve. The note? The performance? The intention behind it? Or something more like an atmosphere — the specific gravity of that moment, in that room, under those particular circumstances, which will never exactly recur?

I've stopped apologizing for the rooms. They're in the recordings now, the way rooms always are — embedded in the reverb tails, the noise floor, the way certain frequencies bloom and others disappear. The album would not exist without them. The wrong rooms were the right rooms. They always are.
